validation note

Predicting which BloodHound paths actually succeed, and which evade detection

A posture-conditioned world model, validated against real attack execution on the GOAD Active Directory forest. It ranks defense-conditioned attack outcomes on networks and postures it never trained on.

method world model conditioned on defensive posture lab GOAD forest on real Windows Server oracle 240 real-execution environments

01The problem

BloodHound tells you which attack paths exist. Given a SharpHound collection, it computes the reachable edges: who can kerberoast whom, which access-control entry gets you GenericAll over a Domain Admin, which session lands you on a path to the domain controller. That graph is correct and it is useful. But it answers a reachability question, not an outcome question.

It does not tell you whether a given path succeeds under the defenses actually deployed on that network: a rotated service-account credential, a service principal restricted to AES-only encryption, a segmented subnet. And it does not tell you whether walking that path trips an alert or sails past the security monitoring unseen. Two paths with identical BloodHound reachability can have completely different real outcomes once you account for posture and detection.

That gap, between "a path exists" and "this path works and nobody sees it," is the thing a defender actually needs to prioritize remediation. It is the thing this work measures.

02The approach

A posture-conditioned world model. Instead of treating the Active Directory graph as static, we condition the prediction on the defensive posture of the environment: the controls a defender has deployed. The model takes a graph plus a compact posture vector and predicts, per path, two independent quantities: the probability the attack succeeds, and the probability it is detected.

The posture vector captures the defensive controls that change real outcomes, each as a normalized value between zero and one. Conceptually, it covers patch level and credential hardening (for example, whether service principals are restricted to AES-only encryption), the fraction of credentials rotated (which closes off credential reuse), how far constrained and unconstrained delegation has been locked down, detection coverage (logging and rules deployed), and network segmentation. Those are the knobs a defender can actually turn, and they are the inputs the model reasons over.

Success and detection are separate channels. We model them as independent channels. A detection rule never blocks an attack, it surfaces it. An attack that succeeds can be loud (detected) or quiet (undetected); an attack that fails can equally be loud or quiet. The product we care about, the reachable and undetected surface, is exactly the conjunction the defender loses sleep over: paths that both work and go unseen. Keeping the two channels separate is what lets us report a path as "succeeds but you'll catch it" versus "succeeds and you're blind to it."

Why Spearman rank correlation. We score the model with Spearman rank correlation (ρ), not point-prediction error. A defender does not need the model to say "this path succeeds with probability 0.62"; they need it to rank the paths so the worst one is on top. Spearman ρ measures exactly that ranking agreement against the ground-truth outcome ordering, and it is calibration-invariant: a model that is systematically off by a monotone transform still ranks correctly and still earns full credit. That is the right notion of "useful" for triage.

Why pre-registration. Every bar in this work, the ρ thresholds, the held-out protocol, the oracle definition, and the conditions under which a result is declared insufficient, was frozen in a pre-registration and committed before any oracle was run, then never moved. In practice this means the decision rule that turns numbers into a verdict is written down and hashed first, so a passing result cannot be a post-hoc choice of threshold.

03The experiment

The lab. GOAD (Game of Active Directory) on real Windows Server in Azure: three domains, sevenkingdoms.local, north.sevenkingdoms.local, and essos.local, with real cross-domain and cross-forest trusts, and five target machines spanning the three domains. The attacks are real, not simulated: a real Kerberoasting service-ticket request (mapped to MITRE technique T1558.003) and a real credential-use lateral movement over SMB (technique T1078). Detection ground truth comes from the real Windows Security Event Log: event 4769 (Kerberos service-ticket request, the kerberoast signal) and event 4624 (logon, the credential-use signal), read off the live domain controllers.

The 240-environment structure. The verdict rests on 8 held-out postures by 6 topology classes by 5 repeats, which is 240 real-execution environments. The 8 postures were frozen in advance and never seen by the model during training. Each environment is a real attack run against a real domain controller, scored against the real event log: no Monte Carlo, and no structural baseline standing in for execution.

Leave-one-domain-out (LODO). To check that the detection result is not memorized per domain, we hold out an entire domain, derive the detection formula from the remaining domains only, and apply it to the held-out one. If ρ stays above the bar on a domain the formula never saw, the ranking generalizes across domains rather than being fit to each.

Why a real oracle. A simulator can only confirm that the model agrees with the simulator's own assumptions. The whole point is to find out whether the model's predictions survive contact with a real Kerberos stack, real AES-versus-RC4 ticket behavior, real event-log emission, and real trust traversal, none of which a simulator gets to define. The pre-registration designates the real-execution result as the only one that produces the verdict; the simulated cells are explicitly diagnostic.

05Honest limitations

These are not caveats to bury. They are the boundaries of the claim, stated technically.

limitation

(1) Lab forest, not real enterprise Active Directory. GOAD is a realistic, trust-bearing, multi-domain Windows forest, but we built it. It does not have the scale, the organic access-control sprawl, the decade of accreted group nesting, or the messy collection reality of a real enterprise. The model's internal network representation is also a short two-hop window; real forests have three to six-hop chains, which the model handles because the posture controls act at the domain controller and service layer regardless of path depth, but deep multi-hop ranking is an extrapolation, not an interpolation. In our internal validation, roughly 13% of large-graph candidates are flagged as low fidelity for this reason.

limitation

(2) Native and Wazuh logs, not production endpoint detection. The headline detection ρ of 0.975 was measured against native Windows Event Logs. When we move the same model to a real Wazuh security-monitoring deployment (families D and F), detection ρ drops to 0.683 [0.557, 0.809]. This is a real calibration-transfer effect, exactly as pre-registered: the native-log-calibrated detection head over-predicts detection for AES-only kerberoast attempts, which emit no roastable-ticket event under the Wazuh rules. It still clears the ρ≥0.50 bar, and family E (native logs on the densified forest) reproduces the higher native behavior at ρ=0.839, confirming the drop is the source change, not the model. But a production endpoint detection and response product is a different detection surface again, and we have not validated against one.

limitation

(3) Per-operating-system detection calibration. Samba is not Windows. The kerberoast detection formula is specific to the operating-system class. Samba and Windows have fundamentally different event-4769 emission behavior, so when the wrong formula is transferred across that boundary, cross-operating-system detection ρ drops to about 0.55. The credential-use channel is fully structural and does not have this problem, at ρ=1.000, but the kerberoast channel must not collapse Samba and Windows domain controllers into one formula. This is disclosed, not papered over.

The three open boundaries, carried on every artifact including every command-line report:

  1. Real enterprise networks, not validated (lab forests only).
  2. Production endpoint detection, not validated; richer Wazuh lab detection partially closes this, but Wazuh is not production endpoint detection.
  3. Temporal and real network drift, not validated; a design-partner export over time remains the decisive test.

06What this means for practitioners

What you can do with this today. Drop in a SharpHound or BloodHound export. The pipeline scores collection completeness first (full, partial, or insufficient, with named re-collection guidance if not full), then returns your attack paths ranked by reachable and undetected risk, each with a confidence interval, then the highest-impact defensive fixes as posture counterfactuals. Counterfactual fixes are validated for credential rotation, delegation locking, and segmentation; anything else is flagged as out of validation rather than claimed. The whole thing runs locally; your collection never leaves your machine.

What you cannot do yet. You cannot treat the detection numbers as validated against your production endpoint detection, and you cannot treat the ranking as validated on enterprise-scale drift over time. Those are boundaries 2 and 3, and they need a design partner with a real network and a real detection stack, not more lab work.

The free tool. A local command-line tool ships alongside this writeup. It runs the exact pipeline above against a BloodHound export you already understand (your own GOAD lab, or a test export), so you can verify the output before you would ever point it at real data. Every report it prints carries the three open boundaries at the bottom.

The evidence. The validation harnesses, the frozen pre-registrations, the per-environment oracle records, and the independent audits are all available with the project. Every ρ in this post is reconstructable from that evidence.

methodology

The model is treated as a frozen black box throughout this evaluation. No result in this post retrained on its own test data. Every bar and protocol was fixed in advance and never moved once an oracle had been run against it.
