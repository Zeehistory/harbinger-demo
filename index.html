Our thesis

A defense that has only read its own logs can recognize the attack it has already survived — and stays blind to the one taking shape.

Machine defense built on signatures and logs inherits both the reach and the limits of its record. It is detection without anticipation, response without rehearsal. It waits at the door for a face it has seen before, while the intrusion that matters is the one assembling itself one quiet hop at a time — a path that exists, fully, before anyone walks it.

A network is not a list of events. It is a space with structure: hosts, trust, privilege, and the seams between what is watched and what is not. An attacker reads that space and improvises through it. To meet them, a defender needs more than memory of the past — it needs the capacity to perceive, anticipate, and improvise within the unfolding dynamics of an intrusion.

We treat the network as a world that can be
simulated forward — and let the model rehearse the breach before it ever happens.

From any foothold, Scry rolls the intrusion forward — thousands of possible moves at once — then collapses that cloud of futures into the single path an attacker is most likely to take, and the sensors that would miss it. Not an alert after the fact. A ranking of the futures worth fearing first.

From record to foresight.

Every security tool stops at one of these. A world model is the only one that reaches the last.

01 log

What happened. The record, always written after the fact. Indispensable, and always one step behind the event it describes.

02 graph

What is reachable. Attack graphs map the structure of what could be touched — but reachability is not likelihood, and a map is not a forecast.

03 world

What could happen. A learned model of the network that rolls forward, exploring how an intrusion would actually unfold across moves it has never seen.

04 foresight

What will — ranked. The cloud of futures collapses to the likeliest path to the crown, and the blind spots along it. The thing worth fixing first.

What we build

One model. Three things it makes safe.

Scry keeps a working model of your environment and uses it the way a strategist uses a war game — to find the move that matters before the adversary does, and to certify the agents we point at real networks.

Foresight

Attack-path ranking

Given a network, Scry ranks where an intrusion is most likely to move next and scores the blind spots your sensors leave uncovered — across topologies it has never seen.

Assurance

Agent certification

A standing benchmark that tests whether an autonomous security agent is competent and safe. It refuses to clear one by default, before it is ever aimed at a live network.

Fidelity

Sim-to-real transfer

Findings are proven to hold from fast simulation to real, instrumented hosts — graded by actual rule-firing on live telemetry, never by synthetic labels.
